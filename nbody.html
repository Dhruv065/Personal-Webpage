Cosmological N-body Simulations

Introduction

Cosmological N-body simulations are the primary tool to study how structure forms in the universe under the influence of gravity. In these simulations, the dark matter distribution is represented by billions of particles, each tracing the motion of a small fraction of the cosmic mass. Starting from initial conditions set by the early universe — consistent with the cosmic microwave background and ΛCDM cosmology — the simulation evolves forward in time using Newton’s laws of motion and gravity.

As the universe expands, small fluctuations in density grow through gravitational instability, eventually forming the nonlinear cosmic web of filaments, sheets, and halos. Within this framework, galaxies and clusters reside inside dark matter halos, whose structure can be directly compared with observations through gravitational lensing, galaxy clustering, and rotation curves.

N-body simulations are especially valuable for testing alternative dark matter models. By adjusting interaction properties, box sizes, and resolution, we can explore how halo profiles and collapse timescales change across different physical scenarios. In my work, I use high-resolution simulations tailored to study intermediate-mass halos, bridging the gap between dwarf galaxies and massive clusters.

Simulation Setup

  • Code: GADGET-2
  • Box size: 500 Mpc/h
  • Number of particles: 1024³
  • Mass resolution: ~5 × 10⁹ M⊙ per particle
  • Halo finder: Rockstar
  • Cosmology: Planck ΛCDM parameters
    • Ωm = 0.307115
    • ΩΛ = 0.692885
    • Ωb = 0.048206
    • h = 0.6777

The smaller 500 Mpc box compared to typical 1000 Mpc runs provides higher mass resolution, ensuring halos of mass 1013–1014 M⊙ are resolved with tens of thousands of particles each.

Effect of Self-Interaction Cross Section

Here we compare stacked density profiles of halos for different self-interaction cross-sections (σ). Increasing σ systematically reduces the central density, demonstrating the coring effect of self-interactions. At larger radii, the profiles remain nearly identical, showing that SIDM mainly alters the inner halo. From the 1000 halos in the mass range 1013–1014 M⊙, we compute stacked density profiles. This improves the signal-to-noise and allows direct comparison with theoretical models and observational probes such as weak lensing. The profiles reveal how SIDM-induced coring becomes more prominent at small radii, while preserving the outer structure.
